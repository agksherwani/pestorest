Toronto has earned its reputation as the raccoon capital of Canada, and for good reason. The city's unique combination of dense urban housing, an extensive ravine system stretching over 300 kilometres, mature tree canopy, and curbside organic waste collection creates ideal conditions for one of the highest raccoon populations per square kilometre in North America. If you own a home in Toronto, the question is rarely whether you will encounter raccoons — it is when. This guide covers everything a Toronto homeowner needs to know about raccoon removal, from recognising the early signs of an attic intrusion to understanding Ontario's wildlife regulations and hiring a professional to resolve the problem humanely and permanently.

Signs You Have Raccoons in Your Attic

Raccoons are nocturnal, so the first sign of an attic intrusion is almost always sound. Homeowners typically notice heavy thumping, scratching, and vocal chittering coming from above the ceiling between dusk and dawn. Unlike mice or squirrels, raccoons are large animals — adults weigh between 5 and 12 kilograms — and their movements are unmistakable. Here are the key signs to watch for:

  • Nighttime noises: Loud thumping, walking, scratching, and vocal sounds (growling, purring, chittering) in the attic or ceiling, typically starting after sunset. During baby season, you may also hear high-pitched crying or mewing sounds from raccoon kits.
  • Visible damage to the roofline: Torn or peeled-back soffit panels, damaged roof vents, ripped shingles near the roof edge, or bent flashing around chimneys and dormers. Raccoons have remarkably dexterous front paws and strong forelimbs capable of prying apart building materials.
  • Droppings on the roof or in the attic: Raccoon droppings are tubular, roughly the size of a small dog's waste, and often deposited in a communal latrine site — a single area where multiple droppings accumulate. These latrines are commonly found on flat roof sections, at the base of trees, or on decks.
  • Stains on ceilings or walls: Urine saturation in the attic can seep through insulation and drywall, creating yellowish-brown stains on interior ceilings below the attic space.
  • Disturbed insulation: Raccoons flatten and compress attic insulation to create nesting areas, significantly reducing its thermal performance. They also tear vapour barriers and shred insulation to build nests for their young.
  • Footprints and grease marks: Raccoon paw prints (resembling small human handprints with five distinct toes) and dark grease smudges around entry points on the fascia or soffit.

If you notice any of these signs, do not attempt to seal the entry point yourself. Trapping a raccoon inside your attic — especially a nursing mother with babies — will cause far more damage and create serious health and odour problems. Instead, contact a licensed wildlife removal company to assess the situation.

Ontario Wildlife Regulations You Need to Know

Before taking any action against raccoons on your property, every Toronto homeowner should understand the provincial laws that govern wildlife removal in Ontario. These regulations exist to protect both animals and homeowners, and violating them carries significant penalties.

The One-Kilometre Release Rule

Under Ontario's Fish and Wildlife Conservation Act (FWCA), any wild animal that is captured must be released within one kilometre of the capture site. This means you cannot trap a raccoon in your Toronto attic and drive it to a rural area, a provincial park, or even a few neighbourhoods away. The one-kilometre rule exists because relocated raccoons suffer extremely high mortality rates — they are unfamiliar with the territory, cannot find food or shelter, and are attacked by resident raccoons defending their established range. Releasing an animal more than one kilometre away without a permit is an offence under the FWCA, with fines of up to $25,000.

DIY Trapping Is Illegal Without a Licence

In Ontario, you need a trapping licence to set live traps for furbearing mammals, which includes raccoons. Homeowners who purchase cage traps from hardware stores and attempt to trap raccoons on their own are technically in violation of the law. Beyond the legal issues, DIY trapping frequently results in capturing non-target animals (skunks, opossums, neighbourhood cats), trapping nursing mothers away from dependent young, and creating situations where stressed animals injure themselves inside the trap.

Baby Season: March Through June

Raccoon baby season in Toronto runs from approximately March through June, with the peak birthing period in April and May. Female raccoons seek out warm, enclosed spaces — attics being the most desirable — to give birth and nurse their litters of three to five kits. The young are immobile for the first several weeks of life and are entirely dependent on the mother. During baby season, wildlife removal requires extra care: one-way doors must be installed with proper timing to ensure the mother has moved her kits out before the entry point is sealed. Reputable wildlife control companies will inspect the attic for babies before installing exclusion devices and may delay certain steps to prevent separating a mother from her nursing young.

The Professional Raccoon Removal Process

Licensed wildlife removal in Toronto follows a standardised process designed to be humane, effective, and permanent. Here is what to expect when you hire a professional company:

Step 1: Inspection

A technician conducts a thorough inspection of the entire roofline, soffits, vents, chimney, and any other potential entry points. The goal is to identify the primary entry point (where the raccoon is actively entering and exiting), any secondary entry points, and any vulnerable areas that could become future entry points. The technician will also check inside the attic for evidence of nesting, the presence of babies, droppings, and damage to insulation, wiring, and ductwork.

Step 2: One-Way Door Installation

The primary exclusion tool for raccoon removal is the one-way door — a device installed over the active entry point that allows the raccoon to push its way out but prevents re-entry. The one-way door is sized appropriately for raccoons and is secured firmly to the building exterior. Once the raccoon leaves to forage at night, it cannot get back in. During baby season, the technician will ensure the kits are old enough to travel with the mother before installing the door, or will hand-remove the babies and place them in a heated reunion box near the entry point so the mother can retrieve them.

Step 3: Exclusion and Screening

While the one-way door handles the active entry point, all other vulnerable areas of the roofline are reinforced with heavy-gauge galvanised steel mesh or custom-fabricated metal flashing. This prevents the raccoon from simply forcing open a new entry point after being excluded from the original one. Common areas that are screened include soffit intersections, roof vents (covered with steel vent caps), plumbing mat boots, gable vents, and gaps at dormers.

Step 4: Door Removal and Final Seal

After a monitoring period (typically 7 to 14 days), the technician returns to confirm the raccoon has vacated the attic. The one-way door is removed and the original entry point is permanently sealed with the same heavy-gauge screening used on the rest of the roofline. The technician performs a final inspection to confirm the attic is clear.

Step 5: Cleanup and Remediation (Optional)

Depending on how long the raccoon occupied the attic and the extent of contamination, homeowners may opt for attic remediation. This includes removing contaminated insulation, sanitising affected areas to neutralise raccoon roundworm eggs and bacteria, deodorising to eliminate urine odour, and re-insulating. While remediation is an additional cost, it is strongly recommended when droppings or urine saturation are extensive.

Common Entry Points on Toronto Homes

Toronto's housing stock is diverse, ranging from Victorian-era homes in Cabbagetown and the Annex to mid-century bungalows in Scarborough and North York to modern infill construction across the city. Each style has its own vulnerabilities, but certain entry points appear consistently across all types:

  • Roof-soffit intersections: The point where the soffit panel meets the roofline is the single most common raccoon entry point in Toronto. Aluminium and vinyl soffits are particularly vulnerable because raccoons can grip the edge and peel the panel back with their front paws. Older Toronto homes with wooden soffits that have begun to rot are even easier to breach.
  • Plastic roof vents: Standard plastic roof vents (for bathroom fans and plumbing stacks) are no match for a determined raccoon. They chew through the thin plastic housing in minutes, gaining direct access to the attic. Replacing plastic vents with galvanised steel vent caps is one of the most effective preventive measures a Toronto homeowner can take.
  • Plumbing mat boots: The rubber gasket (called a plumbing mat or plumbing boot) that seals around the plumbing stack vent where it exits the roof deteriorates over time. Raccoons tear through the degraded rubber to create an opening beside the pipe.
  • Gable vents: Louvered gable vents on older Toronto homes are often made of lightweight aluminium or vinyl and can be pushed or pried open by raccoons. Once the louvers are bent, the opening provides easy attic access.
  • Chimney caps and flashing: Uncapped chimneys and loose flashing around chimney bases are invitations for raccoons. They climb directly down uncapped flues or squeeze through gaps where deteriorating flashing has pulled away from the masonry.
  • Dormer junctions: Where dormers meet the main roofline, small construction gaps are common. These gaps may be invisible from the ground but are large enough for a raccoon to exploit.

Cost of Raccoon Removal in Toronto

The cost of professional raccoon removal in Toronto typically ranges from $400 to $1,200, depending on several factors. Understanding what drives the price helps homeowners evaluate quotes and avoid overpaying or falling for lowball estimates that exclude essential work.

  • $400 to $600 (basic): A straightforward removal involving a single confirmed entry point, one-way door installation, screening of the primary entry after the raccoon vacates, and a basic roofline assessment. This range is typical for newer homes with a single vulnerable spot.
  • $600 to $900 (standard): Removal plus multi-point exclusion screening across the full roofline, including vent caps, soffit reinforcement at intersections, and plumbing boot protection. This is the most common service level for Toronto homes and the one most wildlife companies recommend.
  • $900 to $1,200 (comprehensive): Full-perimeter exclusion, baby removal and reunion during baby season, extensive soffit or fascia repair, and situations requiring multiple one-way doors. Homes with severely deteriorated rooflines or multiple active entry points fall into this range.

Attic remediation (insulation removal, sanitisation, and re-insulation) is typically quoted separately and can range from $1,500 to $5,000 or more depending on the size of the attic and the extent of contamination. Most reputable wildlife removal companies in Toronto include a warranty of one to two years on their exclusion work, covering any re-entry through areas they have screened.

Prevention Tips for Toronto Homeowners

Once raccoons have been excluded from your home, or if you want to prevent an intrusion from ever happening, the following measures will significantly reduce your risk:

  • Secure your green bins: Toronto's curbside organic waste program is a major raccoon attractant. Use the green bin lock provided by the City of Toronto, and do not set bins out the night before collection — wait until the morning of pickup. Store bins in a garage or enclosed area between collection days.
  • Trim tree branches: Raccoons are excellent climbers and routinely access roofs by jumping from overhanging branches. Trim all tree limbs to at least two metres (six feet) from the roofline. Pay particular attention to mature trees along the property line and in the backyard.
  • Inspect your soffits annually: Walk the perimeter of your home each spring and fall, looking up at the soffits with binoculars if needed. Look for gaps, loose panels, signs of chewing or prying, and any areas where the soffit has separated from the fascia board. Catching a small gap before a raccoon finds it is far cheaper than dealing with a full intrusion.
  • Replace plastic roof vents with steel: Proactively swapping standard plastic roof vents for galvanised steel vent caps eliminates one of the easiest raccoon entry points. A roofer or wildlife proofing company can complete this upgrade in a single visit.
  • Cap your chimney: If your chimney does not already have a steel cap with mesh screening, have one installed. An uncapped chimney is an open invitation to raccoons, squirrels, and birds.
  • Eliminate ground-level attractants: Do not leave pet food outdoors. Pick up fallen fruit from fruit trees. Secure compost bins with locking lids. Remove bird feeders or use raccoon-proof feeder designs, as spilled seed attracts raccoons to your yard and orients them to your property.
  • Install motion-activated lights or sprinklers: While not a standalone solution, motion-activated deterrents around known travel routes (fence lines, deck edges, downspout bases) can discourage casual foraging and make your property less attractive to raccoons scouting for den sites.

Toronto Raccoon Facts

Toronto's battle with raccoons is legendary. The city invested over $31 million in raccoon-resistant green bins beginning in 2016, and residents of neighbourhoods like The Annex, Rosedale, High Park, and Riverdale can attest that these animals are remarkably persistent and intelligent. Toronto's ravine system — the Don Valley, Humber River corridor, and the network of creek ravines that thread through midtown and east-end neighbourhoods — provides natural highways that allow raccoons to move deep into residential areas without ever crossing a major road. Homes backing onto ravines in Moore Park, Leaside, and the Bridle Path are especially vulnerable, but raccoon intrusions are common across every part of the city, from Etobicoke semi-detached homes to Scarborough bungalows. If you live in Toronto, raccoon-proofing your home is not optional — it is essential maintenance.
